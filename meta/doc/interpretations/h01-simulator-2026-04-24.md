# H01 Sweep Interpretation — 2026-04-24

> Test artifact: `meta/results/h01-simulator/sweep-2026-04-24.parquet` (144,000 rows).
> Figures: `meta/notebooks/h01_simulator_results.py`.
> Hypothesis: `meta/specs/hypotheses/h01-stochastic-revisiting.md`.
> Plan: `meta/doc/plans/2026-04-24-h01-sweep-and-interpretation.md`.

## Headline finding

H01 is broadly confirmed: every exploration-based policy — Thompson, UCB, and constant-revisit at all tested r values — strictly outperforms hard-gating on mean recall across all noise levels, all three bias regimes, and all budget multiples, with the advantage widening monotonically as evidence becomes noisier, and no revisiting policy hurts recall even in the low-noise regime. UCB (deterministic upper-confidence-bound selection) outperforms Thompson (stochastic sampling) at every noise level and in every bias regime, suggesting the load-bearing mechanism is **uncertainty-guided exploration**, not stochasticity per se — a refinement of P1's literal claim.

## Per-proposition findings

### P1 (existence)

**Verbatim:** "In a simulator that allocates a fixed evidence budget over N propositions of known truth, with per-action signals drawn from a noise model, there exists a parameter regime in which a stochastic-revisiting policy achieves strictly higher final-state recall of the true propositions than a hard-gating policy at equal budget."

P1 is confirmed at every tested noise level and in every bias regime. Thompson sampling achieves a mean recall of 0.555 versus 0.320 for hard-gating (a 23.5-point absolute gap) at the noisiest setting (noise_level = 0.1, all bias models averaged), and 0.907 versus 0.867 at the least-noisy setting (noise_level = 0.8) — a gap that narrows but never closes. UCB's advantage is larger still, reaching 0.674 vs 0.320 at noise_level = 0.1. Figure 1 (recall vs noise level, faceted by bias model) shows that every revisiting policy traces a higher recall curve than hard-gating at every point along the noise axis.

### P2 (realistic regime)

**Verbatim:** "That parameter regime is non-empty for noise levels comparable to published replication-crisis estimates of per-study signal reliability [@Ioannidis2005; @Errington2021; @Niepel2019] in the relevant domains. A hypothesis that only held in implausibly noisy regimes would have limited tooling implications."

P2 is confirmed. The sweep spans five noise levels (p_pos − p_neg ∈ {0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8}), with the two noisiest (0.1 and 0.2, corresponding to p_pos = 0.55/0.60 and p_neg = 0.45/0.40) representing regimes where a positive signal is only marginally more likely from a true proposition than from a false one. These are conservative analogues of real per-study signal reliability in published replication work. At noise_level = 0.2, Thompson achieves 0.642 vs 0.410 for hard-gating (a 23.3-point gap, again averaged across bias models). The advantage is largest precisely in these two most realistic noise settings, not in implausibly extreme regimes. Figure 1 shows the gap is clearly non-negligible across the realistic part of the noise range.

### P3 (mechanism)

**Verbatim:** "The advantage of stochastic revisiting over hard gating is monotonically increasing in early-signal noise within the realistic regime, consistent with bandit-theoretic results on the exploration-exploitation trade-off. This tests that the effect is driven by exploration rather than by an artifact of any particular simulator parameterisation."

P3 is confirmed. The Thompson-vs-hard-gate recall gap, averaged across all three bias models, is: 0.236 at noise_level = 0.1, 0.233 at 0.2, 0.170 at 0.4, 0.105 at 0.6, and 0.040 at 0.8. The sequence is strictly monotonically decreasing as noise decreases (i.e., the advantage grows as evidence becomes noisier). Figure 1 shows this visually as a widening spread between curves at the left side of the x-axis (noisier) vs the right side. Notably, the P3 specification asks whether the shared-bias regime is the most discriminating, and Figure 5 (shared-vs-independent recall delta) shows this is not quite the case: the shared-vs-independent recall delta is small (0.004–0.012 across policies) and not clearly larger in the high-noise regime. The discriminating factor is the noise level, not the bias structure — shared bias does not substantially change the ordering of policies.

### P4 (safety)

**Verbatim:** "Stochastic revisiting does not meaningfully reduce final recall in low-noise regimes relative to hard gating. This matters for tool design: a policy that helps in hard regimes but hurts in easy ones is less appealing than one that helps in hard regimes and is neutral in easy ones."

P4 is confirmed, and more strongly than anticipated. In the low-noise regimes (noise_level = 0.6 and 0.8), no revisiting policy reduces recall below the hard-gate baseline. The gaps at noise_level = 0.6 are: constant-revisit +7.7 to +16.6 points above hard-gating across r ∈ {0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3} (averaging across all r values and bias models gives +12.4 points; the best single r = 0.3 gives +16.6 points), Thompson +8.5 to +12.0 points, UCB +20.8 to +21.6 points, across the three bias models. At noise_level = 0.8, all revisiting policies remain above hard-gating by 3.4 to 11.6 points. The question P4 asked ("does revisiting hurt in easy regimes?") is answered no: revisiting never hurts, and the remaining advantage at low noise is non-trivial. Figure 1 confirms this across all three bias facets.

### P5 (schedule)

**Verbatim:** "The revisiting probability that performs best is a function of estimated per-proposition uncertainty rather than a constant. This is weaker than P1-P3 and could fail even if the main claim holds."

P5 is not confirmed by this sweep. Figure 6 (r-curve: recall vs revisit probability, faceted by bias model) shows that across all three bias regimes and all five noise levels, the maximum-recall revisit probability is uniformly r = 0.3 — the highest value in the tested grid ({0.0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3}). The optimal r does not vary by noise level or bias model within the range tested; the r-curve is monotonically increasing in r throughout. Two interpretations are possible. First, the optimal r may genuinely exceed 0.3 and the grid is simply truncated below the peak, in which case P5 is not falsified but also not measurable. Second, constant r = 0.3 may be near-optimal across all conditions tested, implying the uncertainty-adaptive revisit schedule offers little additional gain. The grid was not extended to r > 0.3 in this sweep, so the distinction cannot be resolved here. Regardless, P5 as stated ("optimal r is a function of uncertainty, not a constant") receives no positive evidence from the data: the optimal constant r appears to be the same across all regimes.

## Alternative-explanation checklist

### Exploration of the prior-sampling distribution, not recovery from noise

This alternative posits that the apparent advantage of Thompson or UCB over hard-gating reflects prior uncertainty representation rather than the mechanism of revisiting gated-out propositions. The test is to run hard-gate with Beta(5,5) priors and compare its recall to hard-gate with Beta(1,1) priors and to Thompson.

Result: hard-gate with Beta(5,5) produces recall that is numerically identical to hard-gate with Beta(1,1) across every (bias_model, noise_level) combination, with zero difference in aggregated recall at any cell. Both start at the same prior mean (0.5 = 1/2 = 5/10), so gate decisions are identical — both policies observe the same signals, update with the same conjugate likelihood increments, and hit the 0.5 gate threshold at the same moments. The prior shape (concentration parameter) affects posterior calibration (Brier scores do differ: Beta(5,5) Brier is higher, indicating over-confidence when posteriors have less room to update) but does not affect allocation decisions. Consequently, the full Thompson-vs-hard-gate recall gap persists unchanged regardless of prior: at noise_level = 0.1, Thompson achieves 0.555 vs 0.320 for hard-gate (either prior). This rules out the "optimistic initialization" framing as an explanation for the effect. The recall advantage of Thompson and UCB is not attributable to prior uncertainty alone; it is attributable to the mechanism of continued sampling of down-weighted propositions.

### Artifact of the Beta-Bernoulli conjugate structure

Not testable in this sweep. The simulator is built on binary Bernoulli observations with Beta-conjugate posteriors. Whether the H01 effect generalizes to continuous-effect-size signal models (e.g., Gaussian evidence with Normal-inverse-gamma priors) cannot be determined from these results. A Gaussian-effect-size variant of the simulator is needed to address this. Recorded as future work.

### Budget-per-proposition vs. noise effect

The alternative suggests that what appears as a noise effect may be a budget-per-proposition effect: if high-noise settings also correspond to low per-proposition information, more budget would resolve the gap. The sweep includes budget_multiple ∈ {2, 8, 32} (actions per proposition), which allows a partial check.

For Thompson vs hard-gate in the independent-noise regime, the gap at noise_level = 0.1 is: 0.080 at budget_multiple = 2, 0.255 at budget_multiple = 8, and 0.303 at budget_multiple = 32. The gap grows with budget rather than shrinking. This is the opposite of what a "budget-per-proposition" explanation would predict: if the effect were solely about information, more budget would allow hard-gate to accumulate enough evidence to match Thompson. Instead, Thompson scales better with budget. In the none (unbiased) regime the pattern is similar: Thompson leads hard-gate by 0.097 at budget_multiple = 2, 0.292 at budget_multiple = 8, and 0.417 at budget_multiple = 32 (noise_level = 0.1). The noise effect is real and amplified, not explained away, by larger budgets.

### Oracle artifact in regret

The `signal_count_regret` metric is documented as unreliable in the shared-bias regime: when a shared bias shifts a false proposition's effective probability above any true proposition's, the oracle targets the false proposition, yielding zero regret at zero recall. This decorrelation is confirmed in the data: the Pearson correlation between `signal_count_regret` and `recall` is −0.035 in the independent-noise regime and 0.244 in the shared regime, neither of which is a reliable indicator. For the none (unbiased) regime the correlation is 0.314. In the shared-bias regime, a lower regret score can correspond to lower recall if the oracle is mislead.

This writeup treats `signal_count_regret` as a diagnostic only. Recall and Brier are the load-bearing metrics throughout. The finding that Thompson beats hard-gate across all cells is grounded in recall, not regret. The regret results in Table form above (for the shared regime) show Thompson having slightly higher mean regret than hard-gate at noise_level = 0.1 (67.6 vs 65.2) while also having higher recall (0.555 vs 0.330), confirming the decorrelation is present and that using regret as a corroborating signal would be misleading.

## Calibration check

Figure 2 (Brier score vs noise level) and Figure 3 (reliability diagram) jointly address whether continuous beliefs are well-calibrated. The per-regime picture differs by policy:

- **UCB**: lower (better) Brier than hard-gate in 14 of 15 (bias_model × noise_level) cells. The single exception is independent, noise=0.1, where UCB Brier (0.246) is 0.003 above hard-gate (0.243) — a negligible difference at the noisiest, hardest-to-calibrate setting. At every other noise level and in every other bias regime UCB cleanly dominates on calibration.

- **constant_revisit**: lower Brier than hard-gate across all 15 cells (including independent, noise=0.1 where the margin is ~0.0001 — essentially tied).

- **Thompson**: lower Brier than hard-gate in the `none` regime at every noise level (diffs ranging from −0.005 at noise=0.8 to −0.021 at noise=0.4), and in the `shared` regime at noise levels 0.1 through 0.6 (diffs −0.004 to −0.012); but in the `shared` regime at noise=0.8, Thompson is marginally worse (+0.0005, essentially tied). In the `independent` regime, Thompson is uniformly worse than hard-gate at every noise level tested — gaps of +0.002 to +0.008 Brier points (noise=0.2: Thompson 0.225 vs HG 0.223; noise=0.4: Thompson 0.186 vs HG 0.183; noise=0.6: Thompson 0.152 vs HG 0.146; noise=0.8: Thompson 0.121 vs HG 0.112; noise=0.1: Thompson 0.248 vs HG 0.243). These gaps are small in absolute terms — a few thousandths — and should be read alongside the recall data: Thompson achieves substantially higher recall in the independent regime despite this slight calibration penalty. The pattern is consistent with Thompson's stochastic exploration recovering more marginal propositions whose posteriors remain genuinely uncertain; it does not indicate systematic miscalibration. The claim "Thompson always improves Brier" is not supported, but the failure mode is regime-specific and modest.

At noise_level = 0.8 in the independent regime, UCB achieves a Brier of 0.074 vs 0.112 for hard-gate — a 38-point proportional improvement in calibration, concurrent with substantially better recall.

The hard-gate Beta(5,5) variant produces substantially worse Brier scores despite identical recall: at noise_level = 0.8, averaged across all three bias models, Brier = 0.170 vs 0.109 for hard-gate Beta(1,1). This is calibration degradation without accuracy loss — the Beta(5,5) prior makes posteriors more anchored and slower to update, so final beliefs are systematically more uncertain (wider) than the data warrant when the posterior has converged. This is the calibration effect of over-concentration rather than over-confidence in the usual sense.

Taken together, the Brier results support D-003 (continuous beliefs as a load-bearing design principle): UCB cleanly dominates hard-gate on calibration; constant-revisit matches or beats hard-gate everywhere; Thompson improves calibration in the none and (mostly) shared regimes but introduces a small calibration penalty in the independent-bias regime. The interpretive frame is that uncertainty-guided exploration (UCB) is strictly superior on calibration, while Thompson's stochasticity introduces a small, regime-specific trade-off — higher recall in independent bias at the cost of slightly worse Brier. This actually reinforces the headline finding that the load-bearing mechanism is structured uncertainty-guided exploration rather than stochasticity per se.

## UCB comparison

UCB outperforms Thompson on recall at every noise level and in every bias regime. The gap between UCB and Thompson (averaged across bias models) is: 0.119 at noise_level = 0.1, 0.126 at 0.2, 0.129 at 0.4, 0.107 at 0.6, and 0.071 at 0.8. UCB also achieves lower Brier scores than Thompson in most cells, particularly at higher noise levels. In the independent-noise regime at noise_level = 0.4, UCB reaches 0.868 recall vs Thompson's 0.717 — a 15-point gap between two revisiting policies that both substantially outperform hard-gating.

This comparison has a specific interpretation: Thompson sampling is a stochastic exploration mechanism, while UCB is a deterministic one based on upper-confidence bounds. If the H01 effect were specifically about the stochastic nature of revisiting (the mechanism that H01 nominally proposes), Thompson would match or exceed UCB. The fact that UCB consistently exceeds Thompson indicates that the benefit is "structured uncertainty-based exploration" rather than "stochastic revisiting specifically." UCB's deterministic upper-confidence criterion is more aggressive at directing budget to under-sampled propositions, and the data show this pays off in recall. Thompson's sampling randomness adds variance without a corresponding recall benefit relative to UCB. This is an important nuance: the H01 claim about "stochastic revisiting" is supported at the level of "any exploration mechanism that revisits down-weighted propositions helps," but UCB's deterministic variant of that mechanism performs better than Thompson's stochastic variant in this simulator.

## Follow-up work surfaced

The r-curve (Figure 6) is monotonically increasing across the full tested range r ∈ {0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3}, with no peak visible. The natural next experiment is extending the r-axis to r ∈ {0.3, 0.4, 0.5, 0.6, 0.7, 1.0} to identify where recall saturates or begins to decline. If the peak is above r = 0.5, the finding of "higher revisit is always better" becomes the new question rather than P5's original framing.

UCB dominates Thompson on both recall and Brier. A successor experiment could test whether the Thompson advantage claimed in bandit-theoretic settings materializes at lower budget-per-proposition ratios, or whether UCB's deterministic selection criterion is reliably superior in the binary-signal regime. The UCB exploration constant c = 1.0 was not varied in this sweep; a c-curve analogous to the r-curve would identify the optimal exploration-exploitation trade-off for UCB.

The Gaussian effect-size variant of the simulator is not yet implemented. This is the primary methodological gap: whether H01 holds under continuous-valued signals with Gaussian noise remains untested. The Beta-Bernoulli abstraction is clean but may not reflect the signal structure in literature evidence, where effect sizes and confidence intervals carry more information than binary support/refute signals.

Hard-gate with Beta(5,5) and Beta(1,1) produce identical recall in this sweep, because both start at posterior mean 0.5 and receive identical conjugate updates. The result rules out optimistic initialization as an explanation for the Thompson/UCB advantage but leaves open the question of whether a higher gate threshold (e.g., 0.6 instead of 0.5) would interact differently with prior concentration. A threshold-versus-prior interaction sweep would complete the picture.

The budget-multiple analysis shows that the Thompson-vs-hard-gate gap grows, not shrinks, with budget per proposition. This is the most practically significant finding: in a real system where budget is not severely constrained, the case for revisiting policies is stronger, not weaker.

## Confidence

**P1 (existence):** High confidence within the simulator. The effect is robust across all 144,000 rows, all bias regimes, and all budget multiples. Whether it generalizes to continuous-signal simulators or real workflows is bounded.

**P2 (realistic regime):** High confidence that the advantage holds at noise_level ∈ {0.1, 0.2}, which are defensible analogues of replication-crisis signal reliability. Moderate confidence that the exact noise parameters map to specific real-world workflows; the simulation uses a symmetric signal model that may not capture publication bias or field-specific noise structure.

**P3 (mechanism):** High confidence that the gap scales monotonically with noise within this simulator. The shared-bias regime is not the most discriminating as the spec hypothesized; all three bias regimes show comparable ordering. The mechanism claim (exploration, not artifact) is supported by the budget-multiple analysis and the UCB vs Thompson comparison.

**P4 (safety):** High confidence. The advantage in low-noise regimes is unexpectedly large — revisiting policies do not just avoid harming recall, they actively improve it even when signals are informative. This strengthens the design case for revisiting policies.

**P5 (schedule):** Low confidence in the P5 framing. The r-curve is monotonically increasing through the top of the tested range; whether the true optimum is constant or noise-dependent cannot be determined from this sweep. The claim that optimal r is a function of uncertainty is not supported positively and not falsified either.

**Overall:** The core claim of H01 — that stochastic revisiting policies outperform hard-gating under noisy evidence — is supported by the simulator data. The mechanism consistent with the data is uncertainty-guided exploration, with UCB performing better than Thompson, which suggests that the benefit is not specifically "stochastic" revisiting but rather "exploration-based" revisiting. Whether this generalizes beyond the binary-signal Beta-Bernoulli simulator is the primary open question.
